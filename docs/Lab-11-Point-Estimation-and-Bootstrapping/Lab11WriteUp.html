1 Introduction

When performing point estimation, there isn’t just one right answer. For example, the Method of Moments (MOM) and Maximum Likelihood Estimates (MLEs) aren’t always the same.

One question that comes up, is why we calculate the sample variance as \[S_{n-1}^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i -\bar{X})^2,\] when it is supposed to be the ``average squared distance from the mean, i.e., \[S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i -\bar{X})^2.\]

3 Executive Summary

Summarize the work you’ve done here to provide a brief (200-300 word) summary of the simulation and the results using your work in Lab 11. Your summary should be professional, clear, and all claims should be corroborated with statistical support.

Solution: This simulation lab showed why the sample variance is calculated with a divisor of \((n−1)\) instead of \(n\) to remove bias. We compared both variance estimators across four the distributions of Poisson, Binomial, Normal, and Exponential, using a set true variance of 2. At each distribution we used repeated sampling scaling in size to measure bias, variance, precision, and mean squared error.

Across all distributions, the \((n−1)\) version of the sample variance performed better because it stayed closer to the true variance on average. However, the version that divides by \(n\) consistently underestimated the true variance which showed a clear negative bias. This pattern was the same for every distribution, suggesting the result is general and not dependent on a specific model.

As sample size increased both estimators became more stable. Their variability decreased, and their sampling distributions became more concentrated around the true value of 2. For small samples, the distributions were right skewed, but they became more symmetric as sample size grew, approaching a normal distribution. At large sample sizes, the two estimators became nearly indistinguishable, with just slight discrepancies around their peaks. The bootstrap results that resampled from a single dataset, showed similar overall patterns but were more sensitive to the particular sample used. Different random seeds led to slightly different shapes.

Overall, the \((n−1)\) version of the sample variance is preferred because it removes bias and provides more accurate estimates across all distributions and sample sizes studied.
